Tools Should Disappear

May 2026 · 4 min read

I used to think mastery meant knowing how everything worked. Every layer, every abstraction, every system call. I wanted to understand it all, all the time.

I was wrong. Mastery is building tools so good that you forget they exist.

The disappearing act

Think about your text editor, your terminal, your browser. When they work, you don't think about them. You think about the problem. The tool is just a window between what you want and what happens.

When a tool demands attention, it has failed. Every second you spend thinking about the tool instead of the task is friction. A second here, a second there. Over a career it adds up to weeks.

How to build one

Defaults matter more than features. A tool that works out of the box beats one that needs configuration to be useful. Most people never change defaults, so get those right first.

Errors should explain themselves. Not error codes. Not stack traces. A clear message that says what happened and what to do next.

Match how people think, not how the system works. Users don't care about your database schema or your state machine. They care about getting from here to there without friction.

The stuff that won't shut up

We've all used tools that fight you. Cryptic errors. Undocumented behavior. More time reading the manual than doing the work.

This usually isn't incompetence. The author optimized for the wrong thing. Power, flexibility, elegance of implementation. Instead of the person using it.

My test

Can someone use the tool without thinking about it? Without reading docs, debugging config, or fighting the interface?

If yes, the tool has disappeared. It's background, like a comfortable chair. You don't notice it. You just work.

That's what I want to build. Not impressive tools. Tools that get out of the way.
