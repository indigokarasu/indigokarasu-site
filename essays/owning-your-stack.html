Owning Your Stack

May 2026 · 4 min read

We install frameworks, wire up services, and ship. Faster than ever. But most developers don't know what's happening beneath the abstractions. What the code actually does when it runs.

I don't think that's sustainable.

Abstractions are traps if you're not careful

Every year brings new frameworks, services, platforms. Each one makes development faster and moves you further from the machine. Nobody should write assembly for a web app, but there's a gap between "using abstractions" and "being trapped by them."

Here's the trap. You learn a framework. You get productive. Years pass. The framework changes or dies. Your deep expertise is suddenly worth less than you thought.

This happens because you learned an implementation, not a principle. You know how the framework handles requests. You don't know how HTTP works. When the framework changes, nothing transfers.

The developers who handle these shifts understand what's under the hood. Not the framework. The protocol. Not the garbage collector. The memory model. Not the RPC library. The network.

Why it matters

When something breaks at 2 AM, the person who understands the stack figures it out in minutes. The person who knows the framework is on Stack Overflow hoping for a matching error message.

When performance matters, one person reasons about where time goes. The other guesses.

When something new ships, one person evaluates it in an afternoon. The other needs a tutorial series. Most new tech is old ideas in new packaging.

Getting started

You don't need a CS degree. You need to go one layer deeper than feels comfortable.

Read protocol specs. Not tutorials. The actual RFC for HTTP. It's more readable than you'd expect and it changes how you think about every web app you touch.

Write a simple HTTP server, from scratch, no frameworks. You won't ship it. That's fine. You'll understand what your framework has been doing for you.

Profile everything. CPU, memory, I/O, network. The bottleneck is never where you think.

What this buys you

This isn't about rejecting abstractions. It's about choosing them deliberately. Knowing what they cost and what they return. Understanding what to do when they break.

Go one level deeper. It compounds.
